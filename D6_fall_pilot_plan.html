Quest MI — Fall Pilot Plan

A structured 5-school pilot of the Horizon 1 system, designed to generate the ground truth needed to scale to 20+ schools in spring and to inform the Chipp Bot build in SY26-27 Q1.

🎯
Primary goal
Test whether the H1 automation system detects risk earlier than the current manual approach and reduces Ivan's monitoring time per school.
📊
Secondary goal
Generate the ground truth — failure patterns, compliance rates, teacher behavior — that the Chipp Bot needs as design input before build begins.
⚖️
Design principle
5 schools is small enough to observe closely, large enough to test automation under real variance. Don't scale until the pilot proves the system works.
Pilot school selection — 5 schools, 5 different risk profiles
School A — Returning / Strong
Control: proven implementation
Returning school with a Facilitator Strength = Strong from a prior cycle. Low risk. Tests whether the system correctly identifies a Green site and leaves it alone.
School B — Returning / Average
Control: baseline comparison
Returning school with Average facilitator strength. Some known challenges. Tests whether the system detects drift from a school with a mixed track record.
School C — New / PBL-experienced
New school, strong facilitator
First implementation, but teacher has prior PBL experience. Tests onboarding automation and whether the readiness checkpoint correctly identifies a capable teacher.
School D — New / PBL-inexperienced
New school, new to PBL
First implementation, teacher new to project-based learning — the highest-risk teacher profile per SY25-26 data. Tests whether M3 risk flags fire early enough to make a difference.
School E — High risk
Multiple known risk factors
New school with at least 2 known risk factors (attendance concerns, scheduling complexity, or weak school culture). Tests whether the system catches a Red site before it becomes a failed implementation.

What to ignore for the fall pilot
SMS automation tool
Ivan texts pilot teachers manually. Measure response rates vs. email. Choose a tool based on what the pilot reveals. Don't build before you know what you need.
Claude API integration (auto-summarization)
Ivan manually pastes pulse open-text responses into Claude for Monday summarization. The manual version builds the habit and generates the use cases before automating.
Chipp Bot (teacher or admin)
Begin Chipp.ai technical verification conversations during the pilot. Don't build until H1 data is flowing — the admin bot has nothing useful to say until Airtable is populated.
Student work rubric library (full set)
Start M3 Insight Statement rubric only. The full 6-module library takes time to calibrate well. Don't rush it — a bad rubric is worse than no rubric for the bot's quality detection.
Custom microsite or A360 as LMS
Platform decision requires Steve's assessment and a team discussion. Cannot be designed in isolation. Defer to spring after the pilot generates evidence about signal gaps.
Full 20-school rollout on day 1
Too much surface area for a first test. 5 schools is the right scope to observe closely, debug automations, and generate learning before scaling. Spring rollout builds on fall pilot findings.
Pilot success metrics — what to measure after 4 weeks
Did the pulse form get submitted consistently?
Target: ≥ 70% weekly compliance across 5 schools
Measure: Weekly Pulse Log record count vs. expected submissions. If below 70%, incentive structure or form design needs revision before scaling.
Did the JIT kits get opened and used?
Target: ≥ 60% open rate; at least 1 teacher references content in pulse open text
Measure: Loom view counts + Mailchimp or Gmail link tracking. If kits go unopened, content or timing needs revision.
Did the M3 risk flag fire predictively?
Target: every school rated Red at M3 entry produces an Average or Weak showcase outcome; every Green school produces Average or Strong
Measure: Cross-reference M3 tier at session 11 with final Implementation Outcome field. This is the most important accuracy test in the system.
Did the pacing mapper reduce planning time?
Target: pacing map completed within 48h of being sent; Farah reports time savings vs. prior cycles
Measure: timestamp difference between mapper sent and mapper completed. Ask Farah directly how long pacing maps took to finalize vs. prior cycles.
Did the system catch anything Ivan would have missed?
Target: at least 1 risk flag fires before Ivan would have noticed through manual monitoring
Measure: Ivan logs every Red/Yellow flag that fires and whether he would have caught it without the system. Qualitative — this is the most important learning from the pilot.
How much time did Ivan spend per school per week?
Target: < 30 min/school/week average for Green sites; < 60 min for Yellow; unlimited for Red
Measure: Ivan tracks time manually for 4 weeks. This is the baseline for the automation ROI case when presenting to team for spring scale-up.

What to revisit after 4 weeks — the learning agenda for the fall pilot

Is pulse form compliance above 70%? If not: was it a form design problem (too long, not mobile-friendly), a framing problem (teachers didn't understand why), or a consequences problem (no real incentive to submit)? The answer determines whether we adjust the form, add an incentive, or accept that the Chipp Bot is needed sooner than planned to replace the form.
Did the M3 risk flag fire predictively for School D (new PBL teacher) and School E (high-risk)? If the system correctly identified these as Red before the showcase, it's working. If they went undetected, the flag logic or trigger timing needs recalibration.
Was the missed-submission detection catching real risk, or generating false alarms? False positives (teachers who submitted but Zapier didn't log it) erode trust in the system faster than no alerts at all. Debug immediately if this happens.
Did the JIT kits land well, or were they generic? The M3 kit in particular needs to feel specific enough to be immediately useful. If teachers describe it as vague, the content needs revision before spring.
Did the Monday digest give Ivan the right information in the right format? Or was it too noisy, too sparse, or formatted in a way that required interpretation? The digest design should be refined based on 4 weeks of actual use before scaling.
What's the single thing that fell through the system that shouldn't have? There will be something — the pilot's job is to find it before it happens at 20 schools. Name it explicitly in the 4-week retrospective.
If we ran 70 schools on this system tomorrow, what would break first? Answer this question at the 4-week retrospective. The answer should drive the spring build priorities and the Chipp Bot feature prioritization.
